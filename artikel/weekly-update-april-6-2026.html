Week of April 6th –
what's happened

A ceasefire announcement flips the narrative from risk-off to risk-on. Stocks surge while bonds barely move — and core CPI surprises to the downside. A breakdown of the week's key macro developments across liquidity, rates, the dollar, commodities and risk assets.

Market Regime: Transition (Risk-off → Risk-on)

1. Liquidity

The Fed's balance sheet remains near $6.63 trillion with RMPs ongoing. Net liquidity is under seasonal pressure from the TGA swelling during April tax season, which drains reserves from the banking system — a historically negative backdrop for risk assets. On the other hand, the ceasefire announcement mid-week triggered a sharp easing of financial conditions globally, partially offsetting the mechanical liquidity drain. Global M2 continues to grow modestly, led by China and the Eurozone.

2. Rates

March CPI came in at 3.3% year-over-year — the highest since May 2024 — driven by an 18.9% surge in gasoline prices and a 44.2% jump in fuel oil. Crucially though, core CPI rose only 0.2% month-over-month and 2.6% year-over-year, both slightly below forecast, suggesting the energy shock had not yet meaningfully spilled into underlying inflation.

The 10-year Treasury yield stood at 4.32% by end of week, off its recent highs, as the ceasefire partially unwound the inflation fear premium. The Fed remains on hold — futures markets priced a 99% probability of a pause at the April 28–29 FOMC meeting, with odds of any cut in 2026 down to around 40%.

3. Dollar

The DXY hovered around 99–100, giving back some of its March gains as risk sentiment improved with the ceasefire. Emerging market assets began recovering modestly, though the dollar's strength over recent weeks had already done damage to EM currencies and debt servicing costs. The softer-than-feared core CPI print reduced the urgency for the dollar to reprice higher on a "hotter Fed" narrative, capping DXY upside for the week.

4. Commodities

The ceasefire was the dominant event. Oil prices tumbled more than 10% on Tuesday evening after Trump announced the two-week ceasefire, with WTI crude falling to around $95 a barrel from above $110. Gold stabilized and recovered toward the $4,660–4,800 range, supported by easing rate-hike concerns and reports of a possible diplomatic resolution, partially reversing March's brutal sell-off.

5. Risk Assets — S&P 500, Nasdaq, Bitcoin, Ethereum, Solana

The S&P 500 gained roughly 3.6% on the week as the ceasefire triggered a sharp risk-on move. Bitcoin jumped to $72,700, surging roughly 5% in 24 hours on the ceasefire news, while the move triggered nearly $600 million in leveraged futures liquidations — mostly short sellers — signaling a powerful short squeeze.

Ethereum outperformed, rising 8.1% on the week to $2,360, with the ETH/BTC ratio recovering from multi-year lows. Solana added 2.2% to $85. Despite the equity surge, long-end Treasury yields barely moved and gold held near $4,800 — suggesting bond and gold markets were more skeptical about whether the ceasefire would hold.

My Observation

The most interesting split this week was between equities and bonds. Stocks exploded higher on the ceasefire as if the war was over, while the 10-year yield barely moved. That divergence tells me equity markets were pricing hope, while bond markets were pricing reality — that a two-week pause is not a resolution, and inflation isn't going away overnight.

The core CPI miss was the one genuinely positive macro surprise: it suggests that so far, the energy shock hasn't infected underlying price dynamics. That's the key variable to watch going forward.

Open Question

What exactly is the transmission lag between an energy shock and core inflation? The March data suggests it hasn't hit yet — but how many months does history suggest we have before gasoline prices at $4+ start showing up meaningfully in services and food?
