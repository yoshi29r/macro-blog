Week of April 13th –
what's happened

Ceasefire optimism meets overbought equities. A breakdown across liquidity, rates, the dollar, commodities and risk assets — plus an open question on whether Ethereum's on-chain divergence from price signals a genuine rotation or a lagging indicator.

Market Regime: Risk-on / Transition

1. Liquidity

Net liquidity conditions remain tight seasonally as the TGA continues building through tax season. However, the ceasefire extension and falling oil prices are providing a meaningful tailwind via improved financial conditions — lower energy prices reduce inflation expectations, which in turn keeps long-end yields from rising further and supports risk assets. Global M2 in dollar terms is benefiting slightly from the DXY pulling back from its March highs.

2. Rates

The 10-year Treasury yield briefly tested below 4.25%, reversing the sell-off that peaked near 4.48% at the height of the conflict. The trend toward lower yields is becoming more established as oil prices retreat, reducing inflation expectations. The FOMC minutes from the March meeting, released mid-week, reinforced the Fed's cautious posture — seven committee members still project zero cuts in 2026, but with oil off its highs and core CPI contained, the narrative is gradually shifting from "fear of hikes" back to "one cut possible in H2."

3. Dollar

The DXY retreated further from its March peak near 101, pulling back toward 99 as risk appetite recovered and the oil-driven safe-haven bid for the dollar unwound. This is incrementally positive for EM assets and for Bitcoin, which historically benefits from a weaker dollar and looser global financial conditions. The dollar remains overvalued on a purchasing-power-parity basis, and further weakness is broadly expected by major institutions as the year progresses.

4. Commodities

Oil stayed volatile but generally lower, with Brent hovering around $95 as the naval blockade of the Strait remained in place but ceasefire optimism capped upside. Gold held near $4,800, consolidating after the March crash and the subsequent recovery. The gold market's refusal to sell off again — despite equities surging — suggests it is no longer purely driven by rate fears, and is beginning to reassert its safe-haven role as geopolitical uncertainty persists.

5. Risk Assets — S&P 500, Nasdaq, Bitcoin, Ethereum, Solana

U.S. stocks posted strong gains for the third straight week, with the Nasdaq gaining 6.84% on the week and the S&P 500 pushing toward new all-time highs, reaching as high as 7,147. The Nasdaq Composite posted its 13th straight higher session — its longest positive streak since January 1992 — and the S&P 500 rose 4.5% for the week, with RSI levels hitting 73–74, firmly in overbought territory.

Bitcoin climbed to $74,935 on Thursday, up 5.4% on the week. Ethereum reached $2,360, with the ETH/BTC ratio recovering meaningfully from multi-year lows as on-chain activity surged — network transactions hit a record 200.4 million in Q1 and stablecoin supply reached an all-time high of $180 billion. Solana traded near $85.

My Observation

The rally has become increasingly top-heavy. The S&P 500 Equal Weight Index rose only 8% from its March lows while the cap-weighted S&P 500 rose 12.2% over the same period — meaning the recovery is being driven by mega-caps, not broad market participation. That's a classic warning sign. Combined with RSI at 73–74 and the Strait of Hormuz still technically closed, the market feels like it's priced for perfection on a peace deal that isn't signed yet.

Open Question

Ethereum's on-chain metrics — record transactions, record stablecoin supply — are diverging positively from price, which has severely underperformed Bitcoin this cycle. Is this the beginning of a genuine ETH rotation, or are on-chain activity metrics a lagging indicator being driven by stablecoin activity that doesn't necessarily translate into price appreciation?
