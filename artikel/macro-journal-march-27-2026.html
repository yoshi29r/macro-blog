Macro Journal March 27, 2026 4 min read

Macro Journal -
March 27, 2026

Turbulent times - the Fed faces a potential leadership change, dollar strength is tightening financial conditions, and oil remains stuck above $110. Here are this week's key observations.

Turbulent times - not just recently, but no less important for that reason. The Fed faces a change in leadership this year: Jerome Powell is set to be replaced by Kevin Warsh, a governor currently considered hawkish and one of the Fed's most vocal critics of expansive monetary policy and its inflated balance sheet. This could mark a fundamental shift in course — for the Fed, and by extension, the global economy.

Liquidity

RRPs continue to decline, the TGA rose slightly, and the Fed's balance sheet remains unchanged. Net liquidity is therefore behaving neutrally this week.

Rates

The yield on US 2-year Treasuries continues to rise, while the 10-year remains broadly stable — a combination that suggests investors still hold relatively stable growth expectations at this point.

Dollar

The DXY continues to strengthen — running contrary to the stated wishes of the current U.S. administration, which has done more or less everything in its power to weaken the dollar, boost exports, and stimulate the domestic economy. The fact that the opposite is happening — the dollar appreciating after a period of weakness — signals that financial conditions are quietly tightening again.

Commodities

Gold is recovering slightly after a weaker phase — always a sign of uncertainty among investors, as gold remains the go-to "safe haven" asset during periods of market stress. Brent crude oil barely moved this week and currently trades around $110. The Iran situation, and particularly the Hormuz bottleneck, remains unresolved and continues to block any path toward meaningfully lower oil prices.

Markets / Risk Assets

The S&P 500 and Nasdaq are down roughly 3% since the start of the week. Bitcoin and Ethereum have followed suit.

Conclusion

Risk assets continue to show resilience despite relatively high interest rates and a strong dollar. This suggests liquidity conditions remain broadly favourable — likely driven by declining reverse repo balances and stable government spending. Markets currently appear to be more driven by liquidity than by interest rates.
