Week of April 8th, 2026 -
what's happened

From the Fed's balance sheet to a striking divergence between gold and oil - a breakdown of this week's key macro developments across liquidity, rates, the dollar, commodities and risk assets.

Liquidity

The Fed's balance sheet stands near $6.63 trillion, after ending its QT program in November and shifting to "Reserve Management Purchases" (RMPs) — buying roughly $40 billion a month in T-bills since December to keep reserves ample. RRP balances have essentially drained to zero, meaning that buffer no longer exists as a liquidity cushion.

A key watch item going into April is the TGA: tax season typically causes the Treasury's cash account to swell by $175–400bn, which drains reserves from the banking system and tightens net liquidity. Using the standard formula (Fed Balance Sheet – TGA – RRP), net liquidity is therefore under seasonal pressure heading into mid-April — at exactly the moment markets are already stressed. Global M2 remains expansionary on a year-over-year basis, driven by China and the Eurozone, but the dollar strength of March partially offsets that impulse for dollar-denominated assets.

Rates

The 2-year UST yield closed March at 3.79%, while the 10-year rose to 4.32% — both driven by the inflation shock from the energy price spike. The yield curve is steepening, but not for good reasons. The short end (2Y) is stuck because the Fed isn't cutting rates, and the long end (10Y) is rising because investors are worried about inflation staying elevated due to the oil shock — meaning the gap is widening from the top, not the bottom.

Dollar

The DXY gained roughly 2.5% in March, breaking back above 100. The cost of servicing dollar-denominated debt for emerging economies spiked as a result, putting EM currencies under significant pressure. Importers were hit hard, while energy-exporting EMs like Norway were notable beneficiaries.

Commodities

The talk of the town was and remains Brent. It climbed back above $110 after a brief pullback from its peak of $112, registering its strongest monthly gain since the 1970s (+63%) as threats to logistics through the Strait of Hormuz injected a persistent geopolitical risk premium.

Gold, on the other hand, counterintuitively dropped ~11.6% in March despite the geopolitical chaos. As surging Treasury yields raised the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding assets, forced selling dominated over safe-haven buying. By end of week, it had recovered towards $4,670.

Markets / Risk Assets

The S&P 500 closed its fifth consecutive losing week — a streak not seen since 2022 — ending around 6,368. The VIX closed at 31, firmly in Extreme Fear territory. Crypto was no exception to the risk-off trend: BTC sitting around ~$80k, Ethereum dropping below $2k, and Solana trading near $78 — all suggesting the broader bearish trend remains intact despite some technical stabilization signals.

Conclusion

This week's most striking observation is the divergence in behaviour between gold and oil. Both so-called "hard assets" should benefit from geopolitical uncertainty — yet they moved in completely opposite directions in March. As explained above, this was likely caused by markets primarily reacting to inflation expectations, not fear per se.

The question is whether this dynamic flips when the narrative shifts from "inflation shock" to "growth shock" — which seems to be exactly what started happening at the end of the week, as bond yields pulled back and gold regained some ground.

Open Question of the Day

What is the exact mechanism by which dollar strength suppresses global liquidity — and at what DXY level does it start to materially tighten financial conditions for EM economies and crypto? Is it mainly through the cost of USD-denominated debt, through reduced M2 growth in dollar terms, or something else?
